Retail Sales Forecasting Across 45 Stores

Developed a modular forecasting system to predict weekly department-level sales across 45 stores. The pipeline incorporates time-based regression, dimensionality reduction, and post-processing adjustments to model retail behavior with seasonality and promotional effects.

Project Goals

The objective was to forecast two months of weekly sales for every store-department combination using historical data. Reliable forecasts help optimize inventory and staffing, especially during holiday peaks.

Dataset & Evaluation

This project is based on the Walmart Store Sales Forecasting competition hosted on Kaggle. The dataset contains historical weekly sales data from 45 Walmart stores across various departments, spanning from February 2010 to October 2012. Each record includes the store ID, department number, date, weekly sales, and a binary flag indicating if the week includes a major holiday.

Sales forecasts were evaluated using Weighted Mean Absolute Error (WMAE), where four key holidays—Super Bowl, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, and Christmas—are weighted five times higher than regular weeks. This metric emphasizes the importance of holiday-aware predictions in real-world retail planning.

Time-Based Cross-Validation:

To ensure realistic evaluation, we used 10 time-based folds. Each fold trained on a growing window of historical data and forecasted the subsequent two months of weekly sales across all store-department pairs. For example, Fold 1 trained on data from Feb 2010–Feb 2011 and predicted sales from Mar–Apr 2011, while Fold 10 predicted Sept–Oct 2012 using all prior data.

Model Evolution

We fit separate linear models for each store-department combination, recognizing that different departments behave differently across stores. This granularity helped us avoid overfitting to general trends and capture local effects like store-specific promotions or regional seasonality. This approach allowed us to capture localized trends while keeping the modeling interpretable and scalable.

  • We began with a time-based regression: Weekly_Sales ~ Yr + Wk, which achieved a WMAE of 1658, showing promise.
  • Adding a quadratic trend term Yr² helped model nonlinear effects and lowered WMAE to 1626.
  • To generalize better, we collapsed future test years (like 2012) into the most recent training year, improving performance to 1618. Removed after SVD was. applied
  • Applied SVD to denoise sales signals across stores → WMAE 1588
  • Smoothed predictions during holiday weeks (rolling blend) → WMAE 1559

Feature Engineering & Noise Reduction

To handle sparse department-store combinations and denoise irregular signals, we applied Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) across each department’s Store × Date sales matrix. SVD was used to compress noisy department-level sales patterns by projecting them onto a low-dimensional space. This allowed us to retain dominant trends while filtering out idiosyncratic fluctuations that might mislead the regression model. Keeping only the top 8 singular components suppressed overfitting while capturing core seasonal and structural signals.

Holiday Handling

Holiday periods (weeks 49–52) often caused overconfident spikes. To account for these, we applied a post-prediction smoothing using a rolling average: 85% current + 15% previous week. This adjustment softened volatility and better reflected realistic sales transitions.

Store-Level Behavior Clustering

To understand structural differences across stores, we clustered them based on standardized weekly sales curves. The resulting clusters captured different operational rhythms. Some had sharp seasonal cycles, others were steadier or more volatile. This informed decisions about segmentation and modeling granularity.

Figure: Standardized Weekly Sales by Store Cluster (Agglomerative, k=4)

Department-Level Behavior Clustering

We repeated clustering on departments and uncovered similarly distinct groupings. Some departments followed retail norms with a holiday peak; others maintained flat or noisy patterns. This supported our decision to denoise department-level data and avoid applying a one-size-fits-all forecasting method.

Figure: Standardized Weekly Sales by Department Cluster (Agglomerative, k=4)

Evaluation

  • Used Weighted Mean Absolute Error (WMAE) with higher weights for Super Bowl, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, and Christmas weeks.
  • Evaluated across 10 sequential test folds spanning different time periods.
  • Final model achieved an average WMAE of 1559.5, outperforming the benchmark of 1580.
  • Executed on Apple M1 Pro (16GB RAM).
